Project Overview

This project explores how armed conflicts affect children’s right to education. Using global time-series data (2010–2024), we compare conflict-affected contexts with stable ones and track how out-of-school rates evolve over time. The goal is to turn complex evidence into clear visuals that highlight an often-invisible form of human suffering: lost learning and lost futures.

Data Sources & Methodology

Primary Sources

  • Share-primary-school-age-out-of-school (source: UNESCO UIS) — percentage of children out of school

  • Number of ongoing conflicts by type (based on UCDP/PRIO & OWID processing) — summed into a total conflicts measure

Data Characteristics

  • Time Period: 2010–2024

  • Geographic Coverage: 6 conflict-affected and 6 stable comparison countries

  • Indicators:
    out_of_school_pct — share of primary-school-age children out of school (UNESCO UIS via OWID)
    num_conflicts — total number of active conflicts per country-year (UCDP/PRIO via OWID)

  • Data Points: ~2,120 after filtering and cleaning

Data Filters

Data from 2010–2024 was selected for the analysis. All available years were used for time-series and distribution analysis, while averages from 2018–2023 were used for the bar ranking to reflect the recent period.

Countries were selected for a balanced regional representation and divided into two groups

  • Conflict-affected countries: Afghanistan (Asia), Syria (MENA), Yemen (MENA), Sudan (Africa), Ukraine (Europe), Palestine (MENA).

  • Stable countries (for comparison): Germany (Europe), Canada (North America), Japan (Asia), Brazil (South America), Kenya (Africa).

Filtering criteria:

  • Years limited to 2010–2024 (to ensure overlap across both datasets)

  • Only records with valid education data retained

  • Conflict status assigned as:
    Conflict if total number of conflicts > 0, otherwise Stable

The merged dataset provides a comprehensive, longitudinal view of how armed conflicts affect access to primary education.By combining UNESCO’s out-of-school statistics with UCDP/PRIO conflict records from 2010 to 2024, it captures both the scale and persistence of educational disruption across time and regions.The balanced sample of conflict-affected and stable countries allows for meaningful comparison — showing how war consistently raises the percentage of children excluded from school.Through this lens, education becomes a measurable indicator of human suffering, linking data to the lived consequences of instability.
