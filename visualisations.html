Comparing Countries and Contexts

Conflict vs Stable — where do children miss school most?

Here we compare countries across time to see how conflict correlates with school exclusion. We summarize levels, trends, and relative shares to reveal systematic gaps between conflict and stable settings. Together, these views show both the scale and persistence of the education crisis during war.

chart

Bar chart — average (2018–2023)

This bar chart ranks countries by the average out-of-school rate in recent years (2018–2023). Conflict-affected nations (e.g., Sudan, Syria) sit far above stable peers (e.g., Germany, Japan), highlighting how violence systematically disrupts schooling.

chart

Grouped bar — yearly by country (2010–2024)

Each country’s bars show year-by-year out-of-school rates. The contrast between Conflict and Stable contexts persists over time, with slower recoveries and higher volatility in conflict settings.

chart

Heatmap — countries × years (2010–2024)

Darker cells mean more children out of school. Conflict zones cluster with sustained high values, while stable countries remain near zero, making the temporal footprint of conflict immediately visible.

chart

100% stacked bar — share of contexts per year

For each year, the chart shows the proportion of selected countries experiencing conflict versus those that are stable. It links shifts in the global conflict landscape to the education gap

chart

Waffle chart — global averages by context

On average, children in conflict settings are several times more likely to be out of school than those in stable contexts, summarizing the overall burden of conflict on learning

What the distributions reveal

Beyond averages — the full spread of exclusion

Averages can hide the shape of the problem. These distribution charts show the spread, skew, and outliers of out-of-school rates. Conflict settings don’t just have higher central values — they also show wider dispersion, meaning more frequent and severe extremes.

chart

Histogram (Conflict vs Stable)

This histogram compares frequency across bins of out-of-school rates. Conflict observations pile up at higher percentages and show a heavier tail, indicating more severe and recurrent exclusion.

chart

Violin plot (by context, 2010–2024)

Each violin displays the full distribution for Conflict vs Stable. Conflict violins are taller and thicker at higher values, capturing both greater inequality and a higher typical level of exclusion.

chart

Boxplot (by context, 2010–2024)

Medians and IQRs confirm the gap: conflict settings have a higher center and wider spread. Whiskers and outliers show extreme episodes of school loss during escalation periods.

Tracking the Flow of Crisis

Analyzing the collective burden of lost learning over time

These visualizations move beyond static averages to show the temporal dynamics of exclusion. We examine how the volume and severity of out-of-school rates shift annually, revealing the persistent nature of conflict’s impact. The focus here is on the collective trend and the continuous flow of children missing education.

chart

The Flow of Exclusion: Conflict vs Stable Rate Volume (2010–2024)

This Streamgraph visualizes the overall volume and dominance of out-of-school rates attributed to conflict versus stable contexts over time. The flowing, organic shapes emphasize the cumulative burden of conflict, highlighting persistent structural gaps in educational access.

chart

Country Timelines: Tracking Out-of-School Rates (2010–2024)

This visualization breaks down the global trend into individual country trajectories. Each small line chart shows the year-by-year evolution of exclusion rates, providing granular insight into national resilience and the immediate impact of conflict onset or de-escalation on learning.

chart

Evolution of Educational Inequality: Density Shift Over Time (2010-2024)

This Ridgeline-style plot illustrates how the distribution of extreme out-of-school rates in conflict-affected contexts has shifted over selected years (e.g., 2010 vs 2023). The movement of the density curve reveals whether education crises are becoming more acute or more widespread over the period.

chart

Shifting Fortunes: Country Ranking by Exclusion Rate (2010–2024)

This Bump Chart visualizes the competitive landscape of educational vulnerability by tracking each country's rank over the years based on its median out-of-school rate. The crossing lines highlight rapid declines in stable countries or persistent high-ranking crises in conflict zones.

Mapping the Geography of Lost Schooling

Spatial patterns and long-term shifts in access to primary education

The choropleth and change maps reveal where educational exclusion is concentrated and how it has evolved from 2010 to 2024, highlighting persistent high-risk regions and trajectories of improvement or decline.

chart

Change Map (2010 → 2024) — Evolution of Out-of-School Rates

This map highlights the change in out-of-school percentages from 2010 to 2024 for each country. Positive values indicate worsening access to education, while negative values reflect improvement. The map reveals how conflict escalation or recovery has shaped long-term educational outcomes.

chart

Choropleth Map — Education Exclusion Intensity

This choropleth map shows the average percentage of primary-school-age children out of school in each country between 2010 and 2024. Darker shades indicate more severe or persistent educational disruption. Conflict-affected countries consistently show higher exclusion levels compared to stable nations.

chart

Proportional Symbol Map — Conflict Burden and Education

The proportional symbol map visualizes the average number of active conflicts in each country by scaling the size of circles. Color intensity reflects average out-of-school rates. Countries experiencing intense or long-lasting conflict tend to display both larger markers and higher exclusion rates.

The Stability Divide: Order vs. Chaos

Tracing the diverging trajectories of education in peace and war

These visualizations expose the deep systemic rift between stable and conflict-affected nations. While the Sankey diagram contrasts the erratic, high-variability outcomes of war zones with the consistency of stable regions, the Network diagram reveals distinct clusters, mathematically proving how conflict isolates countries and fragments educational access.

chart

Sankey diagram - contrast between conflict-affected and stable countries

The Sankey diagram reveals a clear contrast between conflict-affected and stable countries. Conflict countries show high variability, with education outcomes distributed across all exclusion categories, including a substantial number of cases in the High and Medium levels. This reflects the unstable and unpredictable impact of war on schooling. In contrast, stable countries are tightly concentrated in the Low exclusion category, confirming consistent and reliable access to primary education across the years.

chart

Network diagram - Global Education Access Clusters

The network diagram is based on the last available year of data for each country, ensuring that all eleven countries are represented without missing values. Countries are connected when their out-of-school rates differ by 12 percentage points or less, resulting in meaningful clusters. Stable countries (Germany, Japan, Canada, Brazil, Palestine) form a tight low-exclusion cluster with very small differences. Conflict-affected countries such as Kenya, Ukraine, and Syria form a medium-exclusion cluster. Sudan stands out as an isolated node with no edges due to its extremely high exclusion rate, highlighting the severe educational crisis caused by prolonged conflict. This structure clearly separates stable and conflict-affected nations and reinforces the impact of war on access to education.

About This Project

This project visualizes the impact of armed conflicts on children's right to education using global data (2010–2024), comparing out-of-school rates in conflict-affected and stable regions.
